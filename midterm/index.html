Life of a Starfish

Starfish, commonly known as sea stars, are fascinating marine invertebrates that have thrived in the Earth's vast oceans for over 450 million years. Despite their name, they are not actually fish but belong to the echinoderm family, which also includes sea urchins and sand dollars. These unique creatures possess a remarkable water vascular system that powers their hundreds of tiny tube feet, allowing them to grip surfaces with incredible strength. Because they lack a centralized brain and traditional blood system, they rely on filtering seawater to transport vital nutrients throughout their bodies. Their ability to survive in environments ranging from freezing polar depths to warm tropical tide pools is a testament to their evolutionary resilience and complex biological design.

As keystone predators, starfish play a vital role in maintaining the health and balance of underwater ecosystems by controlling the populations of species like mussels and clams. One of their most famous biological traits is the ability to regenerate lost limbs; in some rare instances, a completely new starfish can grow from just a single severed arm. This incredible power of regeneration makes them a subject of great interest to scientists studying cellular growth.
